# Photosynthesis

Photosynthesis is the process by which green plants and some other
organisms use sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide to produce glucose
and oxygen. It is the primary mechanism by which the energy of the
sun enters the biosphere and is essential for the survival of nearly
every other living organism.

The process takes place primarily in the chloroplasts of plant cells.
Chloroplasts contain the green pigment chlorophyll, which absorbs
light energy in the red and blue regions of the visible spectrum.

## The Light-Dependent Reactions

The light-dependent reactions occur in the thylakoid membranes of the
chloroplast. They use solar energy to split water molecules
(photolysis), releasing electrons, hydrogen ions, and oxygen as a
by-product. The captured energy is stored in two molecules: adenosine
triphosphate (ATP) and nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate
(NADPH).

## The Calvin Cycle

The Calvin cycle occurs in the stroma of the chloroplast. It does
not require light directly, although it depends on the ATP and NADPH
produced by the light-dependent reactions. The cycle fixes carbon
dioxide into a three-carbon sugar called glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate
(G3P), which the plant uses to build glucose, starch, and cellulose.

## Net Equation

The net chemical equation of photosynthesis is:

    6 CO2 + 6 H2O + light energy -> C6H12O6 + 6 O2

For each glucose molecule produced, six molecules of carbon dioxide
and six molecules of water are consumed and six molecules of oxygen
are released. The reaction is endothermic and is driven by the
absorbed light energy.
